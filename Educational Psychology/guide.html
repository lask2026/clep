SECTION I: THE BEHAVIORAL PERSPECTIVE (11%)

Core Premise: Learning is a relatively permanent change in behavior resulting from experience. Behaviorists focus only on observable behavior, ignoring internal mental states.

1.1 The Pioneers & Foundations

  • John B. Watson: The father of behaviorism. Famous for the "Little Albert" experiment, proving fear could be conditioned.
  • Edward Thorndike: Proposed the Law of Effect.
  • Definition: Behaviors followed by favorable consequences become more likely; behaviors followed by unfavorable consequences become less likely.
  • Significance: This laid the groundwork for Skinner’s operant conditioning.

1.2 Classical Conditioning (Ivan Pavlov)

Learning through association. A neutral stimulus becomes capable of eliciting a response because it was repeatedly paired with a stimulus that naturally causes the response.

Key Term

Definition

Exam Example

Unconditioned Stimulus (UCS)

The thing that naturally triggers a response.

Loud noise; Food; A puff of air.

Unconditioned Response (UCR)

The natural, unlearned reaction.

Flinching; Salivating; Blinking.

Neutral Stimulus (NS)

A stimulus that initially does nothing.

A bell; A specific teacher's face.

Conditioned Stimulus (CS)

The previously neutral stimulus that now triggers a reaction.

The bell (after pairing).

Conditioned Response (CR)

The learned reaction to the CS.

Salivating at the bell; Anxiety when seeing the teacher.

Key Classical Conditioning Processes:

  1. Generalization: Responding to stimuli similar to the CS.
  • Example: A child bitten by a large dog becomes afraid of all dogs, even small friendly ones.
  1. Discrimination: Responding only to the specific CS and not similar ones.
  • Example: A student is afraid of the math teacher but not the history teacher.
  1. Extinction: The weakening of the CR when the CS is presented without the UCS.
  • Example: The teacher stops giving pop quizzes (UCS) after the bell (CS). Eventually, the bell no longer causes anxiety (CR).

1.3 Operant Conditioning (B.F. Skinner)

Learning in which voluntary behavior is strengthened or weakened by consequences (antecedents →  behavior →  consequence).

A. The Quadrant of Consequences (CRITICAL EXAM AREA)

Remember: "Positive" = Add; "Negative" = Remove/Subtract.

  1. Positive Reinforcement: Adding a pleasant stimulus to increase behavior.
  • Ex: Praise, stickers, bonus points.
  1. Negative Reinforcement: Removing an unpleasant stimulus to increase behavior.
  • Ex: "If you finish your work, you don't have to do homework tonight." (Removing the "bad" homework increases the "finishing work" behavior).
  • Ex: Putting on a seatbelt to stop the annoying car beep.
  1. Positive Punishment (Presentation Punishment): Adding an unpleasant stimulus to decrease behavior.
  • Ex: Detention, extra chores, scolding.
  1. Negative Punishment (Removal Punishment): Removing a pleasant stimulus to decrease behavior.
  • Ex: Taking away a cell phone, time-out (removing from fun environment).

B. Schedules of Reinforcement

How often and when reinforcement is given determines how fast a behavior is learned and how fast it disappears (extinction).

  • Continuous Reinforcement: Reinforcing every correct response. Best for teaching new behaviors.
  • Intermittent Schedules (Best for maintaining behavior):
  • Fixed Ratio (FR): Reward after a set number of responses.
  • Pattern: High rate of response with a pause after the reward.
  • Ex: Earning a free pizza after reading 5 books.
  • Variable Ratio (VR): Reward after a changing/unpredictable number of responses.
  • Pattern: Very high, steady rate. Most resistant to extinction.
  • Ex: Slot machines; Calling on students randomly.
  • Fixed Interval (FI): Reward for the first response after a set time period.
  • Pattern: "Scalloped" (Low activity at start, high activity right before reward time).
  • Ex: Weekly spelling tests (Students cram on Thursday night).
  • Variable Interval (VI): Reward for first response after changing time periods.
  • Pattern: Slow, steady response.
  • Ex: Pop quizzes; Checking email.

C. Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) Techniques

  1. Shaping: Reinforcing successive approximations toward a target goal.
  • Use: When a student cannot perform the task at all yet (e.g., handwriting). Reinforce holding the pencil →   then touching paper →  then drawing a line.
  1. Premack Principle ("Grandma's Rule"): Using a high-frequency (preferred) activity as a reinforcer for a low-frequency (less preferred) activity.
  • Ex: "First do your math (low frequency), then you can play on the computer (high frequency)."
  1. Token Economy: A system where tokens (secondary reinforcers) are earned for good behavior and can be exchanged for backup reinforcers (prizes, privileges).
  2. Satiation: Encouraging a behavior so much that the student gets sick of it and stops.
  • Ex: Letting a student throw spitballs until they are tired of it (Risky but tested concept).

SECTION II: THE COGNITIVE PERSPECTIVE (15%)

Core Premise: Learning is an internal mental process. Focuses on memory, thinking, problem-solving, and information processing.

2.1 The Information Processing Model (Atkinson-Shiffrin)

Describes how information enters the mind, is stored, and retrieved.

Step 1: Sensory Register (Sensory Memory)

  • Holds raw sensory data (sights, sounds) for a very brief time (1-3 seconds).
  • Capacity: Very large.
  • Key Process: Attention and Perception are required to move info to the next stage. If you don't pay attention, it's gone forever.
  • Gestalt Psychology: We perceive patterns, not just parts. (e.g., Closure: seeing a broken circle as a whole circle) .

Step 2: Working Memory (Short-Term Memory)

  • The "workbench" of the mind where conscious processing happens.
  • Capacity: Limited. Originally thought to be 7 +/- 2 items; modern research suggests fewer (cognitive load).
  • Duration: 20–30 seconds without rehearsal.
  • Strategies to extend capacity:
  • Chunking: Grouping bits of info into larger, meaningful units (e.g., memorizing 1-9-8-4 as a date "1984" rather than 4 separate digits).
  • Maintenance Rehearsal: Rote repetition (keeping it in loop).
  • Elaborative Rehearsal: Connecting new info to existing LTM (better for learning).

Step 3: Long-Term Memory (LTM)

  • Permanent storage of knowledge.
  • Capacity/Duration: Theoretically unlimited.
  • Types of LTM:
  • Episodic: Personal experiences/events (e.g., your prom).
  • Semantic: Facts, concepts, rules (e.g., $2+2=4$, capital of France).
  • Procedural: "How-to" skills (e.g., riding a bike, typing).

2.2 Metacognition & Self-Regulation

Metacognition: Knowledge about one's own thinking processes ("Thinking about thinking").

  • Components:
  1. Planning: Deciding how much time to spend on a task.
  2. Monitoring: Checking "Do I understand this?" while reading.
  3. Evaluation: Judging "Did that study strategy work?"
  • School Application: Explicitly teaching students to ask themselves questions as they read improves comprehension.

2.3 Forgetting & Memory Errors

Why do we forget?

  1. Interference:
  • Proactive Interference: Old info blocks new info (e.g., You can't learn your new phone number because the old one keeps popping up).
  • Retroactive Interference: New info wipes out old info (e.g., You learn Spanish, and now you can't remember your French).
  1. Decay: Information fades over time due to lack of use.

  2. Serial Position Effect: We remember the beginning (Primacy) and end (Recency) of a list best, and forget the middle.

2.4 Problem Solving & Transfer

  • Algorithm: A step-by-step procedure that guarantees a solution (e.g., a baking recipe or math formula).
  • Heuristic: A general rule of thumb or mental shortcut that usually works but isn't guaranteed (e.g., "guess and check" or "working backward").
  • Functional Fixedness: The inability to use an object/tool for a new function (e.g., needing a hammer and not realizing a heavy shoe could work).
  • Transfer of Learning:
  • Positive Transfer: Learning A helps Learning B (e.g., knowing Latin helps learn Spanish).
  • Negative Transfer: Learning A hinders Learning B (e.g., driving on the right in US hinders driving on the left in UK).
  • Specific vs. General Transfer: Specific is applying skills to similar contexts; General is applying principles to widely different contexts.

SECTION III: COGNITIVE & SOCIAL DEVELOPMENT (15%)

Focus: How children grow, think, and interact with the world.

3.1 Jean Piaget: Cognitive Constructivism

Piaget believed children are "little scientists" who build their own understanding of the world through exploration.

A. Key Mechanisms of Learning

  • Schema: The mental building blocks or "folders" in the brain used to organize information.
  • Adaptation: The process of adjusting schemas to new environments. It happens in two ways:

  1. Assimilation: Fitting new information into an existing schema.
  • Example: A child sees a zebra and calls it a "horse" because it fits their "horse" schema (four legs, big).

  1. Accommodation: Changing an existing schema or creating a new one to fit new information.
  • Example: The child learns that zebras have stripes and horses don’t, so they create a new "zebra" schema.
  • Equilibration: The drive to find a balance between assimilation and accommodation. When we can't explain something (disequilibrium), we are uncomfortable and motivated to learn.

B. Piaget’s Four Stages of Development (Memorize the Order)

Stage

Age (Approx)

Key Characteristics & Milestones

Sensorimotor

0–2 Years

Object Permanence: Realizing objects exist even when unseen (Peek-a-boo). Learning through senses and movement.

Preoperational

2–7 Years

Symbolic Thought: Using words/images to represent objects.

Egocentrism: Inability to see things from another’s perspective.

Centration: Focusing on only one aspect of a situation (e.g., height of glass, not width).

Lack of Conservation: They think a tall, thin glass has more water than a short, wide one.

Concrete Operational

7–11 Years

Conservation: Understanding that quantity stays the same despite shape changes.

Logical Thinking: Can reason about concrete (real) objects.

Seriation: Arranging items in order (small to large).

Formal Operational

11+ Years

Abstract Thinking: Can think about hypotheticals ("What if..."), justice, and future possibilities.

Deductive Reasoning: General rule →  Specific conclusion.

3.2 Lev Vygotsky: Sociocultural Theory

Vygotsky believed learning is social and culturally based. We learn with others first, then internalize it.

  • Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD): The "Magic Middle." The gap between what a learner can do alone (too easy) and what they cannot do at all (too hard). Learning happens here.
  • Scaffolding: Temporary support given by a "More Knowledgeable Other" (teacher/peer) to help the learner in the ZPD. As ability increases, support is removed.
  • Examples: Sentence starters, modeling, graphic organizers.
  • Private Speech: Children talking to themselves while working. Vygotsky viewed this as a good thing (self-regulation), whereas Piaget viewed it as "egocentric" (immature).

Comparison: Piaget vs. Vygotsky

  • Piaget: Development precedes learning (You must be "ready"). Universal stages.
  • Vygotsky: Learning pulls development (Instruction leads growth). Continuous (no stages). Importance of language/culture.

3.3 Erik Erikson: Psychosocial Development

Life is a series of "crises" that must be resolved for healthy personality development. Focus on the school-age stages.

  1. Trust vs. Mistrust (0–1): "Is my world safe?" (Feeding/Comfort).
  2. Autonomy vs. Shame/Doubt (1–3): "Can I do things myself?" (Toilet training).
  3. Initiative vs. Guilt (3–6): "Is it okay for me to do, move, and act?" (Play/Exploration).
  4. Industry vs. Inferiority (6–12 / Elementary): CRITICAL STAGE. "Can I make it in the world of people and things?"
  • School Focus: Children need to feel competent in schoolwork. Failure leads to inferiority.
  1. Identity vs. Role Confusion (12–18 / Adolescence): "Who am I and where am I going?"
  • School Focus: Peer relationships are dominant. Exploring roles/ideologies.

3.4 Lawrence Kohlberg: Moral Development

How we reason about right and wrong.

  1. Pre-conventional (Self-Interest):
  • Stage 1: Punishment/Obedience ("I won't cheat because I'll get caught").
  • Stage 2: Personal Reward ("I'll help you if you help me").
  1. Conventional (Social Laws/Rules):
  • Stage 3: Good Boy/Nice Girl ("I want people to like me").
  • Stage 4: Law and Order ("The law is the law. If everyone cheated, chaos would ensue").
  1. Post-conventional (Universal Principles):
  • Stage 5: Social Contract ("Laws can be changed if they are unjust").
  • Stage 6: Universal Ethics (Conscience above law; e.g., Gandhi/MLK).

SECTION IV: MOTIVATION (10%)

Focus: The "Why" behind behavior.

4.1 Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic

  • Intrinsic Motivation: Doing an activity for its own sake (enjoyment, curiosity).
  • Extrinsic Motivation: Doing an activity for a reward or to avoid punishment (grades, money).
  • The Overjustification Effect: If you give an extrinsic reward for something a student already loves doing intrinsically, their intrinsic motivation may decrease.
  • Trap: Don't reward a student for reading if they already love reading!.

4.2 Attribution Theory (Bernie Weiner)

How students explain their successes and failures. This determines future effort.

Attributions have three dimensions:

  1. Locus: Internal (Me) vs. External (Teacher/Test).
  2. Stability: Stable (Always true) vs. Unstable (Can change).
  3. Controllability: Controllable (I can fix it) vs. Uncontrollable (I can't fix it).

The "Best" Attribution for Learning:

  • Internal + Unstable + Controllable: "I failed because I didn't study the right way." (I can change this next time).

The "Worst" Attribution (Learned Helplessness):

  • Internal + Stable + Uncontrollable: "I failed because I am stupid." (Nothing I do will matter, so I quit) .

4.3 Goal Orientation

  • Mastery Goals (Learning Goals): Focus on improving, learning new skills, and understanding. Errors are viewed as part of learning.
  • Performance Goals: Focus on looking good, getting high grades, or beating others. Errors are viewed as failure.
  • Performance-Avoidance: Avoiding work so you don't look "dumb."

4.4 Self-Efficacy (Albert Bandura)

  • Definition: Belief in one's capability to organize and execute a specific task.
  • Self-Efficacy vs. Self-Concept:
  1. Self-Concept: "I am good at math" (General perception).
  2. Self-Efficacy: "I can solve this specific quadratic equation" (Task-specific confidence).
  • Four Sources of Self-Efficacy:
  1. Mastery Experiences: Past success (Most powerful).
  2. Vicarious Experiences: Seeing a peer succeed ("If she can do it, I can too").
  3. Social Persuasion: "You can do it!"
  4. Physiological State: Feeling calm vs. anxious.

4.5 Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs

Students cannot learn if basic needs are not met.

  • Deficiency Needs (Must be met first): Physiological (Food/Sleep) →  Safety →  Belonging/Love →  Esteem.
  • Growth Needs: Cognitive (Knowledge) →  Aesthetic →  Self-Actualization (Full potential).
  • Exam Tip: If a student is sleeping in class, check for physiological needs (hunger/home life) before checking for motivation or learning disabilities.

  1. Scenario: A teacher helps a student solve a math problem by asking "What is the first step?" and then letting the student finish.

SECTION V: INDIVIDUAL DIFFERENCES (17%)

Focus: Intelligence, Special Education, and how students differ.

5.1 Intelligence Theories

Defining "intelligence" is controversial. The CLEP tests specific theorists.

A. Charles Spearman: General Intelligence (g)

  • Proposed that intelligence is a single, general factor (g) that underlies all mental abilities.
  • Key Idea: If you are good at math, you are likely good at reading too.

B. Howard Gardner: Multiple Intelligences

  • Proposed 8 distinct intelligences (frames of mind) that are independent of each other.
  • Shutterstock

* **Linguistic:** Word smart (Writer, Lawyer).

* **Logical-Mathematical:** Logic smart (Scientist, Accountant).

* **Spatial:** Picture smart (Architect, Artist).

* **Musical:** Music smart (Composer).

* **Bodily-Kinesthetic:** Body smart (Athlete, Surgeon).

* **Interpersonal:** People smart (Teacher, Salesperson).

* **Intrapersonal:** Self smart (Psychologist).

* **Naturalist:** Nature smart (Biologist, Farmer).

  • Classroom Application: Differentiate instruction to hit different "smarts" (e.g., sing a song about math).

C. Robert Sternberg: Triarchic Theory

  • Intelligence has three parts (PAC):
  1. Practical: "Street smarts," adapting to the environment.
  2. Analytical: "Book smarts," problem-solving, breaking down information.
  3. Creative: "New ideas," dealing with novelty.

5.2 Cognitive Styles

  • Field Dependence:
  • Perceives patterns as a whole. Hard to separate details from the background context.
  • Socially oriented; prefers group work and external structure.
  • Field Independence:
  • Perceives separate parts. Good at analyzing details independently of context.
  • Task-oriented; prefers working alone and structuring their own learning.
  • Impulsive vs. Reflective:
  • Impulsive: Responds quickly, makes more errors.
  • Reflective: Takes time to think, makes fewer errors.

5.3 Special Education & The Law

  • IDEA (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act): The federal law guaranteeing a "Free Appropriate Public Education" (FAPE) to students with disabilities.
  • LRE (Least Restrictive Environment):
  • Students must be educated with non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate.
  • Mainstreaming/Inclusion: Placing special ed students in general ed classrooms.
  • IEP (Individualized Education Program):
  • A legal document detailing a student's specific goals, accommodations, and services.
  • Written by a team (Parents, Teachers, Specialists).
  • Section 504:
  • Civil rights law preventing discrimination.
  • Used for students who need accommodations (e.g., ADHD, diabetes) but do not need the specialized instruction of an IEP.

5.4 Exceptional Learners


  • Giftedness: High ability (IQ > 130), high creativity, and high task commitment.
  • Strategy: Acceleration (skipping grades) or Enrichment (deeper content).
  • Learning Disabilities (LD):
  • Average or above-average intelligence but a significant gap between potential and achievement in a specific area (e.g., Dyslexia).
  • Not caused by low IQ or sensory problems.
  • ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder):
  • Issues with attention, impulse control, and hyperactivity.
  • Strategy: Structure, short tasks, immediate feedback.

SECTION VI: TESTING & ASSESSMENT (12%)

Focus: Measuring what students know.

6.1 Types of Assessment

Type

Definition

Example

Formative

Assessing during instruction to guide teaching. Low stakes.

"Thumbs up/down," Exit tickets, Drafts.

Summative

Assessing after instruction to judge final mastery. High stakes.

Final exam, SAT, End-of-unit project.

Norm-Referenced

Compares a student to others (a norm group).

Grading on a curve; SAT ("You are in the 90th percentile").

Criterion-Referenced

Compares a student to a standard or criteria.

Predicts future potential/ability to learn.

IQ Test; SAT (scholastic aptitude).

Achievement Test

Measures what has already been learned.

Final Exam; AP Test.

6.2 Qualities of Good Tests

  • Reliability: Consistency of results.
  • Test-Retest: Taking the test twice yields similar scores.
  • Split-Half: Odd questions score similar to even questions.
  • Validity: Does the test measure what it claims to measure?.
  • Content Validity: Does the test cover the material taught? (A history test shouldn't ask math questions).
  • Predictive Validity: Does the score predict future performance? (High SAT →  High College GPA).
  • The Golden Rule: A test can be reliable but not valid (a broken scale consistently says 0 lbs). But a test cannot be valid unless it is reliable.

6.3 Statistics & Scoring

  • Measures of Central Tendency:
  • Mean: The average. (Sensitive to outliers/extremes).
  • Median: The middle score. (Best when there are extreme outliers).
  • Mode: The most frequent score.
  • Measures of Variability (Spread):
  • Range: Highest score minus Lowest score.
  • Standard Deviation (SD): How much scores vary from the mean.
  • Small SD: Scores are clustered tight (Class is similar).
  • Large SD: Scores are spread wide (Class has mixed abilities).

The Normal Distribution (Bell Curve)

Getty Images

  • Mean = Median = Mode (Center of the curve).
  • 68% of scores fall within +/- 1 SD of the mean.
  • 95% of scores fall within +/- 2 SD of the mean.
  • Example: If Mean = 100 and SD = 15:
  • 68% of people score between 85 and 115.

Interpreting Scores:

  • Percentile Rank: Percentage of people scoring at or below a given score.
  • 90th Percentile: You did better than 90% of the group. (Does not mean you got 90% correct).
  • Stanine (Standard Nine): Scores divided into 9 bands.
  • 1–3 = Below Average.
  • 4–6 = Average (5 is dead center).
  • 7–9 = Above Average.
  • Grade Equivalent: A score of 5.2 means the student performed as well as an average 5th grader in the 2nd month of school.
  • Caution: It does not mean a 2nd grader is ready for 5th-grade work.

SECTION VII: INSTRUCTIONAL STRATEGIES (10%)

Focus: How to teach effectively.

7.1 Teacher-Centered Instruction (Direct Instruction)

  • Definition: Structured, explicit teaching of specific skills or facts. Best for basic skills (math facts, grammar rules) and factual knowledge.
  • Key Components:

  1. Review: Check previous day's work.

  2. Presentation: Explicitly state the objective and present new material in small steps.

  3. Guided Practice: Teacher leads students through problems; checks for understanding immediately.

  4. Independent Practice: Students work alone to achieve automaticity.

  5. Review/Feedback: Correct errors instantly to prevent learning wrong patterns.

7.2 Student-Centered Instruction (Constructivist)

  • Definition: Students actively build (construct) their own understanding. The teacher is a "facilitator" or "guide on the side."
  • Discovery Learning (Bruner): Students are given a problem/situation and must figure out the rules themselves.
  • Guided Discovery: The teacher provides structure and clues (scaffolding) to ensure students reach the correct conclusion.
  • Expository Teaching (Ausubel):
  • Advance Organizers: An intro statement or framework given before the lesson to help students organize new info.
  • Deductive: Moves from general rule →  specific examples.

7.3 Cooperative Learning

  • Definition: Students working in small, mixed-ability groups to maximize everyone’s learning.
  • Two Essential Elements (Exam Trap):
  1. Positive Interdependence: "We sink or swim together." The group needs everyone to succeed (e.g., Jigsaw method).
  2. Individual Accountability: Each student is tested individually. No "hitchhiking" on the group's work.
  • Jigsaw Classroom: Each student becomes an "expert" on one piece of the topic, then teaches their group members. This forces interdependence.

7.4 Bloom’s Taxonomy (Cognitive Domain)

A hierarchy of thinking skills. You must match the assessment to the objective.

  1. Remember (Knowledge): Recall facts (Define, List, Label).
  2. Understand (Comprehension): Explain ideas (Describe, Summarize).
  3. Apply: Use info in new situations (Solve, Demonstrate, Calculate).
  4. Analyze: Break into parts (Compare, Contrast, Categorize).
  5. Evaluate: Justify a stand or decision (Defend, Critique, Judge).
  6. Create (Synthesis): Produce new or original work (Design, Construct, Invent).

SECTION VIII: CLASSROOM MANAGEMENT (10%)

Focus: Creating an environment for learning.

8.1 Jacob Kounin’s Management Skills

  • Withitness: "Having eyes in the back of your head." The teacher knows what is going on in all parts of the room at all times.
  • Example: A teacher continues lecturing while making eye contact with a student passing a note, stopping the behavior without interrupting the lesson.
  • Overlapping: Dealing with two issues simultaneously.
  • Example: Helping a small group while signaling a distant student to sit down.
  • Ripple Effect: Correcting one student's behavior influences others to behave.
  • Example: "I like how Sarah is sitting quietly." (Suddenly everyone sits quietly).
  • Momentum/Smoothness: keeping the lesson moving without abrupt stops or "dangling" activities.

8.2 Preventive Management

  • Rules vs. Procedures:
  • Rules: General standards (Respect others, Be prepared).
  • Procedures: Specific routines for tasks (How to sharpen a pencil, how to turn in papers).
  • Timing: The first few weeks of school are critical for establishing these routines.

8.3 Dealing with Misbehavior

  • Principle of Least Intervention: Use the simplest correction first.
  1. Nonverbal: Eye contact, proximity control (standing near the student).
  2. Verbal Hint: "Everyone should be on page 5."
  3. Direct Consequence: "Please move your seat."
  • "I" Messages: "I feel frustrated when..." (Focuses on the behavior, not the student).
  • Assertive Discipline: Clear expectations, consistent follow-through, and a calm, firm tone.

SECTION IX: RESEARCH DESIGN (5%)

Focus: How we know what we know.

9.1 Types of Research

  • Descriptive Research: Describes the current state of things (Survey, Ethnography, Observation). It cannot prove cause-and-effect.
  • Correlational Research:
  • Measures the relationship between two variables.
  • Correlation Coefficient ($r$): Ranges from -1.0 to +1.0.

  • Positive ($+1.0$): Variables move together (Study time $\uparrow$, Grades $\uparrow$).
  • Negative ($-1.0$): Variables move opposite (Video games $\uparrow$, Grades $\downarrow$).
  • Zero ($0$): No relationship (Shoe size vs. IQ).
  • TRAP: Correlation does not equal causation. Just because ice cream sales and shark attacks are correlated (both happen in summer) doesn't mean ice cream causes shark attacks.
  • Experimental Research:
  • The only method that proves cause-and-effect.
  • Independent Variable (IV): The thing you change/manipulate (Teaching method).
  • Dependent Variable (DV): The thing you measure (Test scores).
  • Random Assignment: Every participant has an equal chance of being in the control or experimental group. This removes bias.
  • Quasi-Experimental: Like an experiment but without random assignment (e.g., comparing Class A vs. Class B).
  • Action Research: Teachers studying their own classrooms to improve their own practice.

9.2 Developmental Studies

  • Longitudinal: Studying the same people over a long time (Years).
  • Pros: Accurate growth tracking. Cons: Expensive, people drop out.
  • Cross-Sectional: Studying different age groups at the same time.
  • Pros: Fast/Cheap. Cons: Cohort effects (Gen Z is different from Boomers regardless of age).

EXAM DAY STRATEGY CHECKLIST

1. The "Perfect" Answer Profile:

  • Student-Centered: If an answer choice involves the student being active, constructing knowledge, or working with peers, it is usually correct over "Teacher lectures".
  • Preventive: Answers that stop problems before they start (rules, routines, room layout) are better than punishment.
  • Least Restrictive: Always choose the intervention that disrupts learning the least (e.g., walk near the student > send them to the office).

2. Time Management:

  • You have 90 minutes for ~100 questions.
  • That is roughly 50 seconds per question.
  • Strategy: Answer the easy ones instantly. Mark the "scenario" questions that require reading paragraphs and come back to them.

3. "Weak List" Review:

  • Review your weak areas (usually Stats or Piaget's Stages) in the parking lot before you go in.
