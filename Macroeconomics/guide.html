Macroeconomics

Reddit Intelligence: Recent Test‑Taker Patterns

Overall Difficulty and “Feel”

  • Test takers generally describe Principles of Macroeconomics as moderate. It is very passable with focused prep, especially if you have seen AP Macro or high‑school econ.
  • Compared to Micro, Macro often feels more conceptual and policy‑focused, with relatively less calculation and more cause‑and‑effect reasoning about the whole economy.
  • Students who are comfortable with graphs (PPC, AD‑AS, money market, loanable funds, Phillips curve, and foreign exchange) and basic formulas usually find the exam straightforward.

Common Prep Strategies

  • Video‑first approach:
  • Jacob Clifford (ACDC Econ) AP Macro videos and his unit summary videos are used heavily. Many test takers prep almost entirely with these and pass Macro CLEP.
  • Structured course plus voucher:
  • Modern States “Principles of Macroeconomics” course (roughly 20–25 hours) is popular. Students watch lecture videos, do quizzes, then take the CLEP using the voucher.
  • Practice‑test‑driven review:
  • REA, Peterson’s, and other practice exams are used to build speed and comfort with graph and scenario questions. Many people say these feel a bit harder than the real exam, which helps create a margin of safety.
  • Time patterns:
  • About 3–4 days of intense study for those with AP Macro or recent econ, often scoring in the 60s (out of 80).
  • Around 5–7 days of solid, focused study for those starting “cold” but motivated; most still pass comfortably.

High‑Yield and Trap Topics

Most frequently mentioned topics:

  • AD–AS and Macroeconomic Equilibrium
  • Determinants of aggregate demand and aggregate supply, short run vs long run, output gaps, business cycle phases, and policy‑driven shifts.
  • Financial Sector and Monetary Policy
  • Money supply, money demand, money market, loanable funds market, real versus nominal interest rates, bank money creation, and Federal Reserve tools.
  • Measurement: GDP, Unemployment, and Inflation
  • Components of GDP, real vs nominal GDP, CPI, GDP deflator, inflation rate, types of unemployment, and costs of inflation.
  • Stabilization Policy and Phillips Curve
  • Fiscal vs monetary policy, automatic stabilizers, government deficits and debt, short‑run vs long‑run Phillips curve, role of expectations.
  • Open Economy and Exchange Rates
  • Balance of payments (current vs financial accounts), appreciation and depreciation, effects on net exports, and links to AD.

Common trap areas:

  • Confusing fiscal and monetary policy tools and which one directly changes the money supply.
  • Mixing up real vs nominal measures (GDP, interest rates, wages) and how inflation affects each.
  • Misreading capital flows and how changes in interest rates affect the value of the currency and net exports.
  • Forgetting that in the short run some prices/wages are sticky, but in the long run they are flexible (which drives both AS and Phillips curve behavior).

Question Style Patterns

  • Heavy use of scenario‑based multiple choice: “If the Fed does X, what happens to interest rates, investment, AD, real GDP, and price level?”
  • Many graph/data interpretation questions: PPC, AD–AS, money market, loanable funds, Phillips curve, and simple tables or price index baskets.
  • Light calculations:
  • GDP with C + I + G + (X − M), price indices and inflation rates, multipliers, unemployment rates, and simple real vs nominal conversions.
  • Conceptual vocabulary questions:
  • Definitions and applications of terms like “crowding out,” “natural rate of unemployment,” “demand‑pull inflation,” “automatic stabilizers,” “balance of payments,” and “real interest rate.”

Complete Study Guide – Macroeconomics

MODULE 1 – Basic Economic Concepts (about 8–12%)

1.1 Scarcity, Choice, and Opportunity Cost

  • Scarcity: Limited resources vs unlimited wants.
  • Every choice involves a trade‑off.
  • Opportunity cost: The next best alternative you give up when you choose something.

Example: You choose to study for an exam instead of working a paid shift. The opportunity cost is the income from that shift.

Signal words: “trade‑off,” “foregone,” “next best alternative,” “marginal.”

1.2 Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)

  • The PPC (or PPF) shows the maximum combinations of two goods an economy can produce with fixed resources and technology.
  • Points on the curve: Efficient (full employment).
  • Points inside: Inefficient (unemployed resources).
  • Points outside: Currently unattainable.
  • Outward shift: Economic growth (more resources or better technology).
  • Inward shift: Loss of resources (war, disaster).

Opportunity cost appears as you move along the curve: how much of one good you give up to gain more of the other.

Signal words: “economic growth,” “idle resources,” “shifts of the PPC.”

1.3 Comparative Advantage, Specialization, and Trade

  • Absolute advantage: Ability to produce more with the same inputs.
  • Comparative advantage: Ability to produce at a lower opportunity cost. This is what determines who should specialize.

For output problems (fixed resources, different outputs):

  • Opportunity cost of good A = (units of other good given up) / (units of good A gained).

Whoever has the lower opportunity cost in a good has the comparative advantage.

Terms of trade:

  • Acceptable terms of trade for a good must fall between the two countries’ opportunity costs for that good.

Signal words: “comparative advantage,” “specialize,” “terms of trade,” “gains from trade.”

1.4 Demand, Supply, and Market Equilibrium

Demand:

  • Law of demand: As price falls, quantity demanded rises (ceteris paribus).
  • Demand shifters:
  • Tastes and preferences
  • Income
  • Prices of related goods (substitutes and complements)
  • Expectations of future prices/income
  • Number of buyers

Supply:

  • Law of supply: As price rises, quantity supplied rises (ceteris paribus).
  • Supply shifters:
  • Input prices
  • Technology
  • Taxes and subsidies
  • Expectations of future prices
  • Number of sellers

Equilibrium:

  • Where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.
  • Surplus: Price above equilibrium; quantity supplied greater than quantity demanded.
  • Shortage: Price below equilibrium; quantity demanded greater than quantity supplied.

Price controls:

  • Price ceiling below equilibrium: Shortage.
  • Price floor above equilibrium: Surplus.

Signal words: “shortage,” “surplus,” “binding price ceiling/floor,” “shift vs movement along.”

Self‑Check – Basic Economic Concepts

  1. Define opportunity cost and give one example.
  2. What does a point inside the PPC represent?
  3. How do you determine which country has a comparative advantage in a good?
  4. What happens when a binding price ceiling is imposed below equilibrium?
  5. Name two demand shifters and two supply shifters.
  6. What does an outward shift of the PPC represent?
  7. Explain the difference between a shortage and a surplus.

Answers:

  1. Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative forgone; example: choosing to study instead of working means lost wages are the opportunity cost.
  2. Inefficiency or unemployment of resources.
  3. Compute opportunity cost of the good in each country and compare; lower opportunity cost means comparative advantage.
  4. A shortage occurs: quantity demanded is greater than quantity supplied at that price.
  5. Demand: income, tastes, prices of related goods, expectations, number of buyers. Supply: input costs, technology, taxes/subsidies, expectations, number of sellers.
  6. Increased productive capacity or economic growth.
  7. Shortage: quantity demanded > quantity supplied (price below equilibrium). Surplus: quantity supplied > quantity demanded (price above equilibrium).

MODULE 2 – Measurement of Economic Performance (about 12–16%)

2.1 Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP is the market value of all final goods and services produced within a country’s borders in a given period.

Expenditure approach:

  • GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)
  • C = consumption
  • I = investment (business capital, new housing, inventory changes)
  • G = government purchases of goods and services (not transfer payments)
  • X = exports
  • M = imports

Nominal vs real GDP:

  • Nominal GDP: Measured in current prices.
  • Real GDP: Measured in constant base‑year prices to remove the effect of inflation.

GDP deflator:

  • GDP deflator = (nominal GDP / real GDP) * 100

2.2 Price Indices and Inflation

Price index (general form):

  • Price index = (cost of basket in current year / cost of basket in base year) * 100

Inflation rate:

  • Inflation rate between year 1 and year 2 = ((index in year 2 − index in year 1) / index in year 1) * 100

Types of inflation:

  • Demand‑pull inflation: Too much spending (aggregate demand increases).
  • Cost‑push inflation: Higher production costs (short‑run aggregate supply decreases).

Costs of inflation:

  • Menu costs, shoe‑leather costs, uncertainty, redistribution between borrowers and lenders when inflation is unexpected.

2.3 Unemployment

  • Labor force = employed + unemployed (who are actively seeking work).
  • Unemployment rate = (number unemployed / labor force) * 100

Types of unemployment:

  • Frictional: Short term, between jobs or entering/re‑entering the labor force.
  • Structural: Skills do not match job requirements; often due to technology changes or globalization.
  • Cyclical: Due to downturns in the business cycle (recession).

Natural rate of unemployment (NRU):

  • The unemployment rate when the economy is at full employment (includes only frictional and structural unemployment, not cyclical).

2.4 Business Cycle

Phases:

  • Expansion
  • Peak
  • Recession
  • Trough

Recessions are periods of declining real GDP and rising unemployment.

Self‑Check – Measurement of Economic Performance

  1. Write the expenditure formula for GDP.
  2. Why is real GDP more useful than nominal GDP for comparing output across years?
  3. A market basket costs 200 in the base year and 250 this year. What is the price index this year?
  4. If the price index was 100 last year and 120 this year, what is the inflation rate?
  5. Define frictional, structural, and cyclical unemployment.
  6. If the labor force is 50 million and 4 million are unemployed, what is the unemployment rate?
  7. What is meant by the natural rate of unemployment?

Answers:

  1. GDP = C + I + G + (X − M).
  2. Real GDP adjusts for changes in price level, so it reflects changes in actual output rather than price changes.
  3. Price index = (250 / 200) * 100 = 125.
  4. Inflation rate = (120 − 100) / 100 * 100 = 20 percent.
  5. Frictional: between jobs; structural: skills do not match job needs; cyclical: due to overall downturn in the economy.
  6. Unemployment rate = (4 / 50) * 100 = 8 percent.
  7. The unemployment rate when the economy is at full employment, with only frictional and structural unemployment.

MODULE 3 – National Income and Price Determination (about 15–20%)

3.1 Aggregate Demand (AD)

Aggregate demand is the total quantity of goods and services demanded at different price levels.

Formula:

  • AD = C + I + G + (X − M)

Why AD slopes downward:

  • Wealth effect, interest rate effect, net export effect.

Shifters of AD:

  • Changes in consumption, investment, government spending, and net exports.
  • For example: tax cuts can increase consumption; lower interest rates can increase investment.

3.2 Aggregate Supply (AS)

Short‑run aggregate supply (SRAS):

  • Upward sloping because some input prices (especially wages) are sticky.

Long‑run aggregate supply (LRAS):

  • Vertical at potential output (full‑employment real GDP).

Shifters of SRAS:

  • Changes in input prices, productivity, taxes or subsidies, and supply shocks.

Shifters of LRAS:

  • Changes in quantity and quality of resources, technology, and institutions.

3.3 Macroeconomic Equilibrium and Gaps

Short‑run equilibrium:

  • Intersection of AD and SRAS determines actual real GDP and price level.

Long‑run equilibrium:

  • AD, SRAS, and LRAS intersect at the same output level.

Output gaps:

  • Recessionary gap: Actual output is less than potential output (real GDP left of LRAS).
  • Inflationary gap: Actual output is greater than potential output (real GDP right of LRAS).

Self‑correction:

  • In a recession, high unemployment puts downward pressure on wages and input prices, shifting SRAS to the right.
  • In an inflationary gap, tight labor markets push wages up, shifting SRAS to the left.

3.4 Multipliers

Marginal propensity to consume (MPC):

  • MPC = change in consumption / change in income.

Marginal propensity to save (MPS):

  • MPS = change in saving / change in income.
  • MPC + MPS = 1.

Spending multiplier:

  • Multiplier = 1 / (1 − MPC)
  • This is the same as 1 / MPS.

Tax multiplier (for a lump‑sum tax):

  • Tax multiplier = −MPC / (1 − MPC)

Example:

  • If MPC = 0.75, then multiplier = 1 / (1 − 0.75) = 4.

Self‑Check – National Income and Price Determination

  1. What is the formula for aggregate demand?
  2. Name two reasons the AD curve slopes downward.
  3. What does LRAS represent?
  4. Describe a recessionary gap in words.
  5. If MPC is 0.8, what is the spending multiplier?
  6. If the government wants to increase real GDP by 100 billion and the multiplier is 5, how much should it increase spending (ignoring crowding out)?
  7. In the long run, how does the economy self‑correct from an inflationary gap?

Answers:

  1. AD = C + I + G + (X − M).
  2. Wealth effect and interest rate effect (net export effect is another).
  3. Potential output at full employment; long‑run productive capacity of the economy.
  4. Real GDP is below potential output; high unemployment; AD and SRAS intersect to the left of LRAS.
  5. Multiplier = 1 / (1 − 0.8) = 5.
  6. 100 / 5 = 20 billion.
  7. Wages and input prices rise due to tight labor markets, shifting SRAS left until output returns to potential.

MODULE 4 – Financial Sector (about 15–20%)

4.1 Money and Its Functions

Functions of money:

  • Medium of exchange
  • Unit of account
  • Store of value

Types of money:

  • Commodity money (has intrinsic value, like gold).
  • Fiat money (value because the government says so).

Measures of the money supply (conceptually):

  • M1: Currency in circulation plus checkable deposits.
  • M2: M1 plus savings deposits, small time deposits, and some money market funds.

4.2 Banks and Money Creation

Fractional reserve banking:

  • Banks keep a fraction of deposits as required reserves and lend out the rest.

Required reserve ratio (rr):

  • Required reserves = rr * deposits.

Simple money multiplier:

  • Money multiplier = 1 / rr

Example: If rr = 0.2, multiplier = 1 / 0.2 = 5.

4.3 Money Market

Graph features:

  • Vertical money supply line (set by the central bank).
  • Downward‑sloping money demand curve.
  • Intersection gives nominal interest rate.

Monetary policy:

  • If the central bank increases the money supply, the money supply curve shifts right, and nominal interest rates fall.
  • If the central bank decreases the money supply, interest rates rise.

4.4 Loanable Funds Market

Graph:

  • Supply of loanable funds (savers) is upward sloping.
  • Demand for loanable funds (borrowers) is downward sloping.
  • Real interest rate on vertical axis; quantity of loanable funds on horizontal axis.

Shifts:

  • Increase in saving shifts supply right, lowering the real interest rate.
  • Increase in investment demand shifts demand right, raising the real interest rate.

4.5 Real vs Nominal Interest Rates

Relationship:

  • Real interest rate ≈ nominal interest rate − inflation rate

Example:

  • If nominal interest is 6 percent and inflation is 2 percent, real interest is about 4 percent.

4.6 Central Bank Tools and the Quantity Theory of Money

Main tools of the Federal Reserve:

  • Open market operations:
  • Buying bonds increases the money supply and lowers interest rates.
  • Selling bonds decreases the money supply and raises interest rates.
  • Discount rate: Interest rate the Fed charges banks; changing it influences bank borrowing.
  • Reserve requirement: Changing it affects how much banks can lend.

Quantity theory of money (equation of exchange):

  • M * V = P * Q
  • M = money supply
  • V = velocity of money
  • P = price level
  • Q = real output

If velocity and real output are stable, changes in M mostly affect P.

Self‑Check – Financial Sector

  1. Name the three main functions of money.
  2. If the required reserve ratio is 0.1, what is the simple money multiplier?
  3. The central bank buys 10 million worth of bonds, and rr = 0.2. What is the maximum possible increase in the money supply in the simple model?
  4. In the money market, what happens to nominal interest rates if the money supply increases?
  5. What is the main difference between the money market and the loanable funds market?
  6. If nominal interest is 5 percent and inflation is 2 percent, what is the real interest rate?
  7. How does selling government bonds affect the money supply and interest rates?

Answers:

  1. Medium of exchange, unit of account, and store of value.
  2. Money multiplier = 1 / 0.1 = 10.
  3. Multiplier = 1 / 0.2 = 5; maximum increase = 10 million * 5 = 50 million.
  4. Nominal interest rates fall.
  5. Money market: supply and demand for money, determines nominal interest rate. Loanable funds market: supply of and demand for savings, determines real interest rate.
  6. Real interest = 5 − 2 = 3 percent.
  7. Selling bonds reduces the money supply and raises interest rates.

MODULE 5 – Inflation, Unemployment, and Stabilization Policies (about 20–25%)

5.1 Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy is the use of government spending and taxation to influence aggregate demand.

  • Expansionary fiscal policy: Increase government spending and/or cut taxes to close a recessionary gap.
  • Contractionary fiscal policy: Decrease government spending and/or raise taxes to reduce inflationary pressure.

Automatic stabilizers:

  • Features like progressive income taxes and unemployment insurance that automatically increase spending or reduce taxes during downturns without new legislation.

5.2 Monetary Policy

Monetary policy is the central bank’s control of the money supply and interest rates.

  • To fight recession:
  • Buy bonds, lower discount rate, or lower reserve requirement.
  • This increases money supply, lowers interest rates, and raises investment and AD.
  • To fight inflation:
  • Sell bonds, raise discount rate, or raise reserve requirement.
  • This decreases money supply, raises interest rates, and reduces AD.

5.3 Phillips Curve

Short‑run Phillips curve (SRPC):

  • Shows inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment in the short run.
  • Movements along SRPC are caused by changes in aggregate demand.

Long‑run Phillips curve (LRPC):

  • Vertical at the natural rate of unemployment.
  • In the long run, there is no trade‑off between inflation and unemployment.

Role of expectations:

  • When people adjust expectations of inflation, the SRPC can shift.

5.4 Inflation Types and Stagflation

  • Demand‑pull inflation: Caused by increases in aggregate demand.
  • Cost‑push inflation: Caused by decreases in short‑run aggregate supply.
  • Stagflation: Combination of high inflation and high unemployment, usually linked to negative supply shocks.

Self‑Check – Inflation, Unemployment, and Stabilization Policies

  1. What are the main tools of expansionary fiscal policy?
  2. Give one example of an automatic stabilizer.
  3. In the short run, what is the effect of expansionary fiscal policy on inflation and unemployment (according to the Phillips curve)?
  4. Why is the long‑run Phillips curve vertical?
  5. What happens to inflation and unemployment when a negative supply shock hits the economy?
  6. Which policy directly changes the money supply: fiscal or monetary?
  7. Briefly define stagflation.

Answers:

  1. Increase government spending and/or cut taxes.
  2. Unemployment insurance or a progressive income tax system.
  3. Inflation increases and unemployment decreases.
  4. Because unemployment returns to the natural rate in the long run regardless of inflation; expectations adjust.
  5. Both inflation and unemployment increase (stagflation).
  6. Monetary policy.
  7. High inflation and high unemployment at the same time.

MODULE 6 – Economic Growth and Productivity (about 5–10%)

6.1 Sources of Long‑Run Growth

Long‑run growth occurs when potential output (LRAS) increases.

Sources:

  • Increases in human capital (education, training, health).
  • Increases in physical capital (machines, infrastructure).
  • Technological progress and innovation.
  • Improved institutions (property rights, stable government, sound financial system).

6.2 Growth Policy

Policies that promote growth:

  • Encouraging education and training.
  • Encouraging savings and investment (including business investment and R&D).
  • Protecting property rights and maintaining political stability.
  • Avoiding excessive deficits that crowd out investment.

Self‑Check – Economic Growth and Productivity

  1. What is the difference between short‑run expansion and long‑run economic growth?
  2. Name three factors that can shift LRAS to the right.
  3. How does investment in human capital affect long‑run growth?
  4. Why can large and persistent government budget deficits be harmful to growth?
  5. On a PPC, how is long‑run economic growth shown?
  6. Give one example of a policy that could increase the growth rate.
  7. Does an increase in AD alone (with no change in LRAS) represent long‑run growth?

Answers:

  1. Short‑run expansion is movement along SRAS due to changes in AD; long‑run growth is a shift of LRAS outward.
  2. More capital, better technology, more or better labor, better institutions.
  3. It makes workers more productive, raising output per worker and potential GDP.
  4. Deficits may raise interest rates and crowd out private investment in capital.
  5. As an outward shift of the PPC.
  6. Investment tax credits, better education funding, R&D support, stronger property rights, etc.
  7. No; it raises current output and prices in the short run but does not increase the economy’s productive capacity.

MODULE 7 – Open Economy: International Trade and Finance (about 9–13%)

7.1 Balance of Payments

Two main accounts:

  • Current account:
  • Trade in goods and services (exports minus imports).
  • Net income from abroad.
  • Net current transfers.
  • Financial account:
  • Net capital flows (purchases of financial assets and real assets across borders).

Relationship:

  • A current account deficit is matched by a financial account surplus, and vice versa.

7.2 Foreign Exchange Market (FOREX)

  • Exchange rate: Price of one currency in terms of another.
  • Demand for a currency: From foreigners who want domestic goods, services, or assets.
  • Supply of a currency: From domestic residents who want foreign goods, services, or assets.

Appreciation:

  • Currency value rises; exports become more expensive; imports become cheaper.

Depreciation:

  • Currency value falls; exports become cheaper; imports become more expensive.

7.3 Capital Flows and Links to Other Markets

  • If domestic real interest rates rise relative to foreign rates, foreign capital flows in, raising demand for domestic currency and causing appreciation.
  • Appreciation tends to reduce net exports and decrease aggregate demand.
  • Depreciation tends to increase net exports and increase aggregate demand.

Self‑Check – Open Economy

  1. What are the main components of the current account?
  2. If a country has a current account deficit, what must be true of its financial account?
  3. How does an appreciation of a country’s currency affect its exports and imports?
  4. What happens to capital flows and the domestic currency if domestic real interest rates rise above foreign real interest rates?
  5. What is the likely effect of a depreciation on net exports and aggregate demand?
  6. In the foreign exchange market, what determines the exchange rate in a floating system?
  7. How does an increase in foreign demand for a country’s assets affect its currency?

Answers:

  1. Trade in goods and services, net income from abroad, and net current transfers.
  2. It must have a financial account surplus (net capital inflow).
  3. Exports become more expensive, imports become cheaper; exports tend to fall, imports tend to rise.
  4. Capital flows into the country and the domestic currency appreciates.
  5. Net exports rise and aggregate demand increases.
  6. Supply and demand for the currency.
  7. Demand for the currency increases, causing it to appreciate.

Exam Strategy & Study Plans – Macroeconomics

Time Management

  • 80 questions in 90 minutes → about 1.1 minutes per question.
  • Aim to complete a first pass in 55–60 minutes and use the remaining time for flagged questions and checking your work.

First Pass vs Second Pass

  • First pass:
  • Answer all straightforward conceptual questions quickly.
  • If a question requires multiple steps or heavy calculation, mark it and move on.
  • Second pass:
  • Return to marked questions and invest more time.
  • Draw quick graphs on scratch paper (AD–AS, money market, loanable funds, Phillips curve, FOREX) and reason through scenarios.

Always guess when unsure; there is no penalty for incorrect answers.

1‑Week Cram Plan – Macro

Day 1 – Module 1 (Basics)

  • Study scarcity, PPC, opportunity cost, comparative advantage, trade, and basic supply and demand.
  • Do 20–30 practice questions on PPC and S–D.

Day 2 – Module 2 (Measurement)

  • Study GDP, unemployment, and inflation. Practice index and rate calculations.
  • Do 20–30 practice questions on GDP, CPI, unemployment, and business cycle.

Day 3 – Module 3 (AD–AS and Multipliers)

  • Learn and practice AD and AS shifts, short vs long run, output gaps, and multipliers.
  • Do 25–35 practice questions on AD–AS and fiscal policy.

Day 4 – Module 4 (Financial Sector)

  • Study money, banking, money creation, money market, loanable funds, and real vs nominal interest.
  • Do 25–35 questions focused on these topics.

Day 5 – Module 5 (Stabilization Policies and Phillips Curve)

  • Study fiscal vs monetary policy, automatic stabilizers, and both Phillips curves.
  • Do 25–35 questions on policy scenarios and inflation‑unemployment trade‑offs.

Day 6 – Modules 6 and 7 + Practice Test

  • Review economic growth, productivity, and open economy topics.
  • Take one full‑length practice test (or equivalent 80‑question set) under timed conditions.
  • Review mistakes carefully.

Day 7 – Targeted Review

  • Focus on weak areas revealed by the practice test.
  • Do 30–40 mixed questions, then do light formula and graph review.

1‑Month Deep‑Dive Plan – Macro

Week 1 – Modules 1 and 2

  • Days 1–3: Basic concepts and PPC, supply and demand.
  • Days 4–6: GDP, CPI, inflation, and unemployment.
  • End of week: 30–40 practice questions on basics and measurement.

Week 2 – Module 3

  • Deep‑dive into AD–AS, multipliers, and output gaps.
  • Redraw AD–AS and practice telling the story of each shock.
  • Short quiz every other day.

Week 3 – Module 4 and 5

  • Study money, banking, and interest rates first.
  • Then link into stabilization policies and the Phillips curve.
  • End of week: 40–60 practice questions on financial sector and policy.

Week 4 – Modules 6 and 7 + Full Practice

  • Early week: Long‑run growth and international trade/finance.
  • Two full practice tests on separate days with full review.
  • Last few days: Mixed review focusing on weak points and graph drills.
