Microeconomics

Reddit Intelligence: Recent Test‑Taker Patterns

Difficulty and Overall Feel

  • Most test takers describe Principles of Microeconomics as moderate to challenging. It is more technical and graph‑heavy than some other CLEPs, but very passable with directed study.
  • Compared to Macro, Micro focuses more on individual markets, firms, and consumers, with many detailed questions on supply and demand, elasticity, cost curves, and market structures.

Common Prep Strategies

  • Jacob Clifford AP Micro playlists and micro unit summaries for all key graphs and concepts.
  • Modern States Principles of Microeconomics for a structured course and voucher.
  • AP Micro resources (Fiveable notes, AP review books) because CLEP Micro and AP Micro align closely in content and models.
  • Practice tests from sources like Mometrix, TestPrepReview, and others to gain speed and familiarity with question styles.

High‑Yield and Trap Areas

Frequently tested:

  • Supply and demand, price controls, and welfare analysis.
  • Elasticity and total revenue relationships.
  • Cost and production concepts: fixed vs variable cost, ATC, AVC, AFC, MC, and relationships between them.
  • Market structures: perfect competition, monopoly, monopolistic competition, oligopoly, and basic game theory.
  • Consumer choice and utility maximization.
  • Externalities and public goods.
  • Factor markets: marginal revenue product and the hiring rule.

Common traps:

  • Confusing shifts vs movements along supply or demand curves.
  • Misinterpreting elastic vs inelastic demand when predicting total revenue changes.
  • Mixing up ATC, AVC, AFC, and MC on graphs.
  • Forgetting that the profit‑maximizing condition is MR = MC in all market structures.

Question Style Patterns

  • Many scenario‑based questions requiring logical reasoning rather than memorization.
  • Heavy use of graphs and tables for cost curves, utility, supply and demand, and externalities.
  • Calculation questions on elasticity, marginal values, profit, and simple algebraic supply and demand equations.
  • Conceptual vocabulary questions on different types of goods, structures, and market failures.

Official Exam Overview & Content Breakdown

Basic Exam Facts

  • Format: Computer‑based, multiple‑choice.
  • Number of questions: About 80 (some unscored pretest items).
  • Time limit: 90 minutes.
  • Recommended credit: ACE recommends 3 semester hours of credit for a score of 50, but each college may differ.

Abilities / Skills Tested

  • Understanding of important microeconomic terms and concepts.
  • Interpretation and manipulation of economic graphs.
  • Interpretation and evaluation of economic data.
  • Application of simple microeconomic models.

MODULE 1 – Basic Economic Concepts (about 8–14%)

1.1 Scarcity, Choice, and Opportunity Cost

  • Scarcity: Resources are limited while wants are unlimited.
  • Opportunity cost: The value of the next best alternative that is given up.

Marginal thinking:

  • Make decisions where marginal benefit is greater than or equal to marginal cost.

Signal words: “trade‑off,” “foregone,” “marginal,” “opportunity cost.”

1.2 Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)

  • Points on PPC: Efficient.
  • Inside: Inefficient/unemployed resources.
  • Outside: Unattainable with current resources.
  • Outward shift: Growth (more resources or better technology).

1.3 Comparative Advantage, Specialization, and Trade

For output tables:

  • Opportunity cost of good A = (units of other good lost) / (units of A gained).
  • The country with lower opportunity cost for a good has comparative advantage in that good.

Terms of trade:

  • Acceptable range is between the two opportunity costs for that good.

1.4 Economic Systems and Incentives

  • Market, command, and mixed economies.
  • Role of property rights and incentives in promoting efficiency and growth.

1.5 Marginal Analysis

  • Continue an activity as long as marginal benefit is greater than or equal to marginal cost.
  • This logic appears in consumer choice and firm hiring decisions.

Self‑Check – Basic Economic Concepts

  1. Define opportunity cost.
  2. What does a point inside the PPC represent?
  3. How do you identify comparative advantage using an output table?
  4. What does an outward shift of the PPC represent?
  5. Give one difference between a market and a command economy.
  6. State the marginal decision rule using marginal benefit and marginal cost.
  7. If Country X gives up 3 units of wheat for each unit of steel and Country Y gives up 5 units of wheat per unit of steel, who has comparative advantage in steel?

Answers:

  1. The value of the best alternative forgone.
  2. Unused or inefficiently used resources.
  3. Compute opportunity cost of each good in each country; lower opportunity cost means comparative advantage.
  4. Increased productive capacity or economic growth.
  5. Market: decisions by individuals/firms and guided by prices; command: decisions centralized by government.
  6. Do more of an activity while marginal benefit is greater than or equal to marginal cost.
  7. Country X (lower opportunity cost of steel).

MODULE 2 – The Nature and Functions of Product Markets (about 55–70%)

2A. Supply and Demand (15–20%)

2A.1 Demand

Law of demand:

  • As price decreases, quantity demanded increases (ceteris paribus), and vice versa.

Demand shifters:

  • Income, tastes, prices of related goods, expectations, number of buyers.

2A.2 Supply

Law of supply:

  • As price increases, quantity supplied increases (ceteris paribus).

Supply shifters:

  • Input prices, technology, taxes/subsidies, expectations, number of sellers, prices of related outputs.

2A.3 Equilibrium and Changes

  • Equilibrium price and quantity where demand equals supply.
  • Surplus: price above equilibrium; quantity supplied > quantity demanded.
  • Shortage: price below equilibrium; quantity demanded > quantity supplied.

2A.4 Price Controls and Quotas

  • Price ceiling (maximum price, binding if below equilibrium) → shortage.
  • Price floor (minimum price, binding if above equilibrium) → surplus.
  • Quota (quantity limit) reduces quantity traded and typically raises price.

2A.5 Elasticity

Price elasticity of demand:

  • Ed = (% change in quantity demanded) / (% change in price)

Classifications:

  • Absolute value of Ed greater than 1: elastic.
  • Absolute value of Ed less than 1: inelastic.
  • Absolute value of Ed equal to 1: unit elastic.

Total revenue:

  • TR = price * quantity

Price change effects:

  • With elastic demand:
  • Price increase → total revenue decreases.
  • Price decrease → total revenue increases.
  • With inelastic demand:
  • Price increase → total revenue increases.
  • Price decrease → total revenue decreases.

Other elasticities:

  • Income elasticity: positive for normal goods, negative for inferior goods.
  • Cross‑price elasticity: positive for substitutes, negative for complements.

2A.6 Surplus, Taxes, and Deadweight Loss

  • Consumer surplus: area below demand curve and above price.
  • Producer surplus: area above supply curve and below price.
  • Total surplus: sum of consumer and producer surplus.

Tax incidence:

  • Economic burden falls more heavily on the less elastic side of the market.

Deadweight loss:

  • Reduction in total surplus when quantity traded is less than the efficient equilibrium (e.g., due to taxes, quotas, price controls, or monopoly).

2B. Theory of Consumer Choice (5–10%)

2B.1 Utility Concepts

  • Total utility (TU): total satisfaction from consuming a good.
  • Marginal utility (MU): additional utility from one more unit.

Law of diminishing marginal utility:

  • As more of a good is consumed, MU eventually decreases.

2B.2 Utility Maximization

Rule:

  • A consumer maximizes utility when marginal utility per dollar is equal across goods and the budget is fully spent.

Equation:

  • MU of good X / price of X = MU of good Y / price of Y

If MUx / Px is greater than MUy / Py, the consumer should buy more of X and less of Y.

2C. Production and Costs (10–15%)

2C.1 Short‑Run Production

  • Total product (TP), marginal product (MP), average product (AP).
  • Marginal product of labor (MPL) is extra output from one more unit of labor.
  • Law of diminishing marginal returns: as more variable input is added to fixed input, MP eventually falls.

2C.2 Cost Concepts

Short‑run cost definitions:

  • Fixed cost (FC): does not vary with output.
  • Variable cost (VC): varies with output.
  • Total cost (TC): TC = FC + VC.

Per‑unit costs:

  • Average total cost (ATC) = TC / quantity.
  • Average variable cost (AVC) = VC / quantity.
  • Average fixed cost (AFC) = FC / quantity.
  • Marginal cost (MC) = change in total cost / change in quantity.

Key relationships:

  • MC intersects ATC and AVC at their minimum points.
  • When MC is less than ATC, ATC decreases; when MC is greater than ATC, ATC increases.

Shutdown rule in the short run:

  • If price is at least equal to AVC, the firm continues to produce.
  • If price is less than AVC, the firm shuts down in the short run.

2C.3 Long‑Run Costs

  • Long‑run average total cost (LRATC) curve shows lowest possible ATC for each output level when all inputs are variable.
  • Economies of scale: LRATC decreases as output increases.
  • Constant returns to scale: LRATC stays the same as output increases.
  • Diseconomies of scale: LRATC increases as output increases.

2D. Firm Behavior and Market Structure (23–33%)

2D.1 Profit, MR, and MC

  • Total revenue (TR) = price * quantity.
  • Profit = total revenue − total cost.

Marginal concepts:

  • Marginal revenue (MR) = change in total revenue / change in quantity.
  • Marginal cost (MC) = change in total cost / change in quantity.

Profit‑maximizing rule:

  • A firm maximizes profit by producing the quantity where MR equals MC.

2D.2 Perfect Competition

Characteristics:

  • Many small firms, identical products, free entry and exit, price takers.

Firm behavior:

  • Market sets the price; firm’s demand curve is horizontal at the market price.
  • In the short run, the firm chooses output where MR (which equals price) equals MC.
  • Profit depends on where price is relative to ATC and AVC.

Long run:

  • Entry and exit drive economic profit to zero.
  • Firms produce at minimum ATC and at allocatively efficient level where price equals MC.

2D.3 Monopoly

Characteristics:

  • One firm, unique product, high barriers to entry, price maker.

Behavior:

  • Downward‑sloping market demand curve.
  • Firm produces where MR equals MC and charges the price on the demand curve at that quantity.

Results:

  • Price is greater than MC.
  • Quantity is less than the competitive output level.
  • Deadweight loss due to underproduction.

Price discrimination:

  • Charging different prices to different consumers for the same good (not based on cost differences).
  • Perfect price discrimination can theoretically eliminate consumer surplus and deadweight loss.

2D.4 Monopolistic Competition

Characteristics:

  • Many firms, differentiated products, free entry and exit.

Short run:

  • Firm behaves like a small monopoly, choosing output where MR equals MC.

Long run:

  • Entry and exit lead to zero economic profit.
  • Firm’s demand curve becomes tangent to ATC at the profit‑maximizing quantity.
  • Excess capacity: firm does not produce at minimum ATC and price is greater than MC.

2D.5 Oligopoly and Game Theory

Characteristics:

  • Few large firms, interdependence, barriers to entry, possible collusion.

Game theory:

  • Use of payoff matrices to analyze choices.
  • Nash equilibrium: each firm’s strategy is best given what the others do; no incentive to change strategy.

Self‑Check – Product Markets

  1. Write the formula for price elasticity of demand.
  2. How does total revenue change when price rises and demand is elastic?
  3. State the utility maximization rule using marginal utility and price.
  4. Define ATC, AVC, AFC, and MC and give their formulas.
  5. What is the profit‑maximizing rule for any firm?
  6. In the long run, what happens to economic profit in a perfectly competitive market?
  7. Why does a monopoly create deadweight loss?

Answers:

  1. Ed = (% change in quantity demanded) / (% change in price).
  2. Total revenue decreases when demand is elastic and price increases.
  3. Choose quantities so that MUx / Px equals MUy / Py for all goods and spend the full budget.
  4. ATC = TC / Q, AVC = VC / Q, AFC = FC / Q, MC = change in TC / change in Q.
  5. Produce the quantity where marginal revenue equals marginal cost.
  6. It goes to zero because entry and exit compete away profits.
  7. It restricts output below the competitive level and charges a higher price, leaving some mutually beneficial trades unrealized.

MODULE 3 – Factor Markets (about 8–14%)

3.1 Derived Demand and MRP

Derived demand:

  • Demand for a factor of production is derived from demand for the final product.

Marginal revenue product (MRP):

  • MRP = marginal product (MP) * marginal revenue (MR).
  • In a perfectly competitive output market, MR equals price, so MRP of labor equals marginal product of labor * price.

3.2 Hiring Rule and Labor Demand

Hiring rule:

  • Hire workers until MRP equals marginal factor cost (MFC).
  • In a competitive labor market, wage equals MFC.

Labor demand curve:

  • The MRP curve is the firm’s labor demand curve.
  • Slopes downward because of diminishing marginal product.

3.3 Labor Supply and Wage Determination

Labor supply:

  • Upward sloping: as wage increases, more workers are willing to supply labor.

Equilibrium wage:

  • Determined by intersection of labor demand and labor supply in the labor market.

Shifters of labor demand:

  • Changes in demand for the product.
  • Changes in worker productivity.
  • Changes in prices of related inputs.

Shifters of labor supply:

  • Changes in population.
  • Changes in preferences for work vs leisure.
  • Changes in alternative opportunities.

Self‑Check – Factor Markets

  1. Why is labor demand called “derived”?
  2. Define MRP.
  3. State the hiring rule in a competitive labor market.
  4. What happens to employment if the market wage rises above the equilibrium wage?
  5. Name two factors that shift labor demand.
  6. Name two factors that shift labor supply.
  7. In a competitive labor market, how is the equilibrium wage determined?

Answers:

  1. Because it depends on the demand for the product that labor helps produce.
  2. Additional revenue generated by hiring one more unit of a factor.
  3. Hire workers until MRP equals the wage (marginal factor cost).
  4. Quantity of labor demanded falls; unemployment may result.
  5. Changes in product demand and worker productivity.
  6. Changes in population and preferences for work vs leisure (or changes in alternative job opportunities).
  7. By the intersection of market labor demand and market labor supply.

MODULE 4 – Market Failure and the Role of Government (about 10–16%)

4.1 Externalities

Negative externality:

  • A cost imposed on third parties (e.g., pollution).
  • Market output is greater than the socially optimal level.

Positive externality:

  • A benefit received by third parties (e.g., vaccination, education).
  • Market output is less than the socially optimal level.

Corrective policies:

  • Negative externalities: taxes, regulations, tradable permits.
  • Positive externalities: subsidies, public provision, or mandates.

4.2 Public Goods and Common Resources

Public goods:

  • Nonrival and nonexcludable.
  • Suffer from the free‑rider problem; private markets underprovide them.
  • Often provided by government and financed through taxes.

Common resources:

  • Rival but nonexcludable (e.g., fisheries).
  • Subject to overuse (tragedy of the commons).

Private goods:

  • Rival and excludable.

Club goods:

  • Nonrival and excludable.

4.3 Antitrust Policy and Regulation

  • Antitrust laws aim to reduce monopoly power and promote competition.
  • Regulation can set prices or output levels for natural monopolies.
  • Goals are to improve efficiency and protect consumers.

4.4 Income Distribution and Equity

  • Governments may use taxes and transfers to redistribute income.
  • Trade‑off between equity (fairness) and efficiency (maximizing total surplus).

Self‑Check – Market Failure and Government

  1. In a negative externality, does the market overproduce or underproduce relative to the social optimum?
  2. Give one policy that corrects a negative externality.
  3. Define a public good and give an example.
  4. Why does the free‑rider problem occur?
  5. What is the purpose of antitrust policy?
  6. How can the government address a positive externality?
  7. What is one trade‑off involved in redistributive policies?

Answers:

  1. Overproduce; quantity is greater than the socially optimal level.
  2. A tax equal to the marginal external cost, regulation, or tradable permits.
  3. A good that is nonrival and nonexcludable; example: national defense.
  4. Because people can enjoy the good without paying, so private firms lack incentives to provide it.
  5. To limit monopoly power, prevent anti‑competitive behavior, and promote more competitive markets.
  6. Subsidies, public provision, or legal requirements that increase consumption.
  7. Redistributive policies may reduce inequality but can introduce distortions that reduce efficiency.

Exam Strategy & Study Plans – Microeconomics

Time Management

  • 80 questions in 90 minutes → about 1.1 minutes per question.
  • Aim to complete a first pass in 55–60 minutes, leaving 30–35 minutes for flagged questions and checks.

First Pass vs Second Pass

First pass:

  • Answer all questions you find straightforward.
  • For time‑consuming ones (complicated graphs, long tables, game theory), mark and skip temporarily.

Second pass:

  • Revisit marked questions.
  • Draw quick S–D, cost, or payoff diagrams as needed.
  • Use formulas (elasticity, profit, MRP) step by step.

Always answer every question; there is no penalty for guessing.

1‑Week Cram Plan – Micro

Day 1 – Module 1 + Basic S–D

  • Study Module 1 basics and then start supply and demand.
  • Do 20–30 questions on PPC, opportunity cost, and basic S–D shifts.

Day 2 – Deep S–D and Elasticity (2A)

  • Study elasticity, total revenue, surplus, tax incidence, and deadweight loss.
  • Do 25–35 practice questions on these topics.

Day 3 – Consumer Choice and Costs (2B and 2C)

  • Study utility, marginal utility per dollar, and cost curves.
  • Do 25–35 practice questions on utility and cost relationships.

Day 4 – Market Structures (2D)

  • Cover perfect competition, monopoly, monopolistic competition, and oligopoly.
  • Do 25–35 questions on structure characteristics, graphs, and efficiency comparisons.

Day 5 – Factor Markets and Market Failure (3 and 4)

  • Study MRP, hiring rule, labor markets, externalities, and public goods.
  • Do 25–35 questions across these topics.

Day 6 – Full Practice Test

  • Take a full‑length practice test under timed conditions.
  • Review mistakes and identify weak topics.

Day 7 – Targeted Review

  • Focus on weaknesses (often elasticity, cost curves, or market structures).
  • Do 30–40 mixed questions and a final review of formulas and key graphs.

1‑Month Deep‑Dive Plan – Micro

Week 1 – Module 1 and Early 2A

  • Build foundations: scarcity, PPC, comparative advantage, and basic S–D.
  • Light practice and diagrams.

Week 2 – Finish 2A and Cover 2B and 2C

  • Elasticity, surplus, taxes, and then utility and costs.
  • Aim for 40–60 questions by week’s end.

Week 3 – 2D Market Structures + First Practice

  • Spend several days on perfect competition and monopoly.
  • Then monopolistic competition and oligopoly with basic game theory.
  • Take a 40–50 question mini‑exam and review thoroughly.

Week 4 – Modules 3 and 4 + Full Practice

  • Study factor markets and market failure.
  • Take at least one full‑length practice test.
  • Use results to target weak areas and run mixed practice until exam day.
